# Founder's Playbook — Restaurant Tech Origin Profiles (Batch 1: POS & Ordering Platforms)

Prepared for **AZ Restaurant Partners**. Goal: reconstruct the true origin/funding/GTM story of leading restaurant-technology companies to extract a repeatable playbook for selling technology to restaurants.

Companies in this batch: **Toast, Square, Lightspeed, SpotOn, TouchBistro, Clover, Revel Systems, Olo, ChowNow, Owner.com, Slice.**

Methodology note: facts below are cross-checked across multiple sources and cited inline as (source: domain). Where a figure is uncertain, unverified, or conflicting between sources, it is flagged explicitly. Nothing here is fabricated; when we could not confirm a number we say so.

---

### Toast

- **What it is / category** — Cloud, Android-based all-in-one restaurant point-of-sale (POS) and management platform (payments, online ordering, payroll, loyalty, lending). The category-defining "restaurant operating system."

- **Founders & backgrounds** — Steve Fredette, Aman Narang, and Jonathan Grimm, three former colleagues from **Endeca**, a Cambridge/Boston enterprise-search and data-management software company that Oracle bought for ~$1.1B in 2011. Their backgrounds were enterprise software, data, and engineering — **not** restaurants (source: en.wikipedia.org; bostonglobe.com; news.mit.edu). At least ~20 ex-Endeca employees went on to found companies; early backing came from ex-Endeca CEO Steve Papa.

- **Founding story** — Founded December 22, 2011, in the Boston area (offices in Cambridge/Burlington, MA). The origin insight came from the founders repeatedly dining out and waiting far too long for the check — they wanted to pay and split via smartphone (source: entrepreneur.com; bostonglobe.com). Their first product was a **consumer** mobile app for paying, splitting checks, loyalty and promotions that would ride on top of restaurants' existing POS. They hit a wall: legacy POS systems (Micros, Aloha) were too closed to integrate. That forced the pivot: rather than sit on top of the POS, **become** the POS — a cloud, Android-based system built for merchants. Founders spent hundreds of hours shadowing restaurant staff to ground the product in real operational pain (source: news.mit.edu; aws.amazon.com).

- **Funding history** — Bootstrapped/seeded early with ~$2M around 2011–2012 from ex-Endeca executives (Steve Papa) (source: matrixbcg.com — treat exact figure as approximate). Institutional rounds began 2015: first tracked round Aug 6, 2015. Bessemer Venture Partners led/entered at Series B (Jan 2016). Lead Edge Capital and Generation Investment Management entered at Series C (2017). Series F in Feb 2020 raised **$400M at a ~$4.9B valuation** (source: tracxn.com; verdictfoodservice.com). Pre-IPO total raised was roughly **$900M+** across ~7 rounds (source: tracxn.com). **IPO: September 22, 2021, on NYSE (ticker TOST) at $40.00/share**, opening near a ~$30B market value (source: sec.gov 424B4; crunchbase; thestreet.com). Current status: **public (NYSE: TOST)**, roughly ~$25–30B market cap range.

- **First customers & early GTM (MOST IMPORTANT)** — The wedge was a **cloud POS that was cheaper, easier, and remotely updatable** versus closed legacy systems, later fused with **integrated payment processing** (the real money engine). GTM was famously **founder-led, in-person, boots-on-the-ground**. Insiders warned the founders that restaurant tech is brutally hard to sell because "you typically have to visit an eatery in person to make the sale," not sell by email/phone — the "cost of selling" was the central worry (source: bostonglobe.com). Toast leaned into it: local Boston field sales, shadowing operators, and an explicit **24/7 support** promise that closed deals with skeptical hospitality groups (Narang recounts pointing to the "24/7 Support" line on the site to win an early large Boston hospitality group). The pitch that resonated: **turn tables faster when busy; drive incremental orders/loyalty when slow.** Within ~3 years they had 1,000+ customers and millions in revenue (source: bostonglobe.com; aws.amazon.com). Pricing model evolved to **hardware + SaaS subscription + payment processing take-rate** — payments became the dominant revenue line (land on software, monetize on payments and fintech).

- **Evolution & pivots** — Consumer app → merchant cloud POS (the foundational pivot). Expanded into payments, online ordering (accelerated massively during COVID-19), Toast Capital (merchant lending), payroll/HR (Toast Payroll), email/loyalty marketing, and guest-facing handheld/kiosk hardware. Scale (as of FY2025): **~164,000 total locations** (up ~22% YoY; +30,000 net adds in 2025, a company record), **FY2025 revenue ~$6.15B** (+24%), **GPV ~$195B**, and **GAAP net income $342M** — now solidly profitable (source: businesswire.com FY2025 results; macrotrends.net). Management targets $5B–$10B ARR over the next decade.

- **Restaurant-specific lessons**
  - The pivot lesson is the biggest: you can't sit on top of restaurant systems — the incumbents are too closed. To win restaurants you often must **own the system of record (the POS)**.
  - **In-person, local, founder-led selling is a feature, not a bug** in restaurants. The high cost-of-sale scares off competitors and becomes a moat once you build the field muscle.
  - **24/7 support is a closing argument.** Restaurants run nights/weekends; downtime = lost revenue. Support is sold, not just delivered.
  - **Monetize on payments, not just software.** SaaS lands the account; the payment take-rate scales with the customer's volume (aligned incentives).
  - **Shadow the operator.** Product credibility came from hundreds of hours on restaurant floors — outsiders earned the right to build by observing.

- **Sources** — en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toast,_Inc.; bostonglobe.com (2023 feature); news.mit.edu/2019/toast-restaurants-0310; entrepreneur.com; aws.amazon.com/blogs/startups; sec.gov 424B4 (IPO prospectus); tracxn.com; businesswire.com FY2025 results; macrotrends.net.

### Square (Block, Inc.)

- **What it is / category** — Payments + POS ecosystem for small/medium merchants; began as a mobile card reader, now a full commerce platform ("Square") inside parent **Block, Inc.** Restaurants are one vertical (Square for Restaurants).

- **Founders & backgrounds** — Jack Dorsey (Twitter co-founder; software/product) and Jim McKelvey (a St. Louis glassblowing-studio owner and serial entrepreneur/engineer). Neither came from restaurants; the seed was a small-merchant payments pain point (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Block,_Inc.; startupsavant.com).

- **Founding story** — Founded February 2009, St. Louis (then San Francisco). The origin insight: McKelvey lost a **~$2,000 sale of a glass faucet** because he couldn't accept a credit card. That "why can't anyone take a card with a phone?" moment produced the first product: a small **square-shaped card reader** plugging into a smartphone's headphone jack, letting anyone accept cards with near-zero setup (source: frederick.ai; inc.com; producthabits.com).

- **Funding history** — First institutional round in 2009 led by **Khosla Ventures** (~$10M) at roughly a $45M valuation; angels included Richard Branson, Marissa Mayer, Biz Stone, and Shawn Fanning (source: cnbc.com; producthabits.com). Multiple later rounds (Sequoia, others). **IPO: November 2015 on NYSE at $9/share (ticker then SQ).** Renamed **Block, Inc.** in December 2021; ticker later changed to **XYZ**. Current status: **public (NYSE: XYZ)**. FY2024: **Square GPV ~$228B, >4M sellers, 5.2B transactions**; Block total gross profit ~$8.89B (2024) (source: en.wikipedia.org; statista.com; coinlaw.io; expandedramblings.com). Note: exact early-round figures vary slightly by source — treat the $10M/$45M as approximate.

- **First customers & early GTM (MOST IMPORTANT)** — The wedge was **radical simplicity + a free reader + no monthly fee**, flat, transparent per-swipe pricing (historically **2.75%**, now **2.6% + 10¢** in-person on the free plan). This was **self-serve, inbound, product-led** growth — the opposite of Toast's field-sales motion. Merchants who could never get a traditional merchant account (micro-merchants, food trucks, markets, artists, small cafés) onboarded themselves in minutes; the free hardware and overnight deposits were the "yes." Square only built a **dedicated restaurant product later**: **Square for Restaurants launched May 8, 2018** — its third vertical POS (after Retail and Appointments) and first for full-service restaurants — retroactively justifying the **2014 acquisition of Caviar** (delivery) which was integrated as first-party ordering (source: cnbc.com; squareup.com press; sec.gov 10-K FY2018).

- **Evolution & pivots** — Reader → Register/Stand hardware → full seller ecosystem (payroll, capital, invoices, Square Online) → Cash App (huge consumer arm) → vertical POS products including **Square for Restaurants** (2018) and Caviar delivery (acquired 2014, later sold to DoorDash in 2019). Rebrand to Block (2021) reflecting bitcoin/fintech ambitions. Restaurants remain one vertical within a much larger payments empire.

- **Restaurant-specific lessons**
  - **Product-led, self-serve onboarding can win the long tail** (food trucks, cafés, micro-merchants) that field sales can't afford to chase — the inverse of Toast's model, but it works at the small end.
  - **Free hardware + transparent flat pricing removes the "merchant account" friction** that historically excluded small food businesses.
  - Coming from **generic SMB payments into restaurants late** meant Square had to bolt on restaurant-specific depth (coursing, KDS, floor plans) years after Toast built it native — a cautionary tale about **restaurant specificity**: full-service restaurants need vertical features, not a horizontal POS.
  - **Delivery/first-party ordering is strategically valuable** (Caviar) but hard to run — Square ultimately divested it.

- **Sources** — en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Block,_Inc.; frederick.ai; startupsavant.com; producthabits.com; cnbc.com; inc.com; squareup.com press releases; sec.gov Square 10-K FY2018; statista.com; coinlaw.io.

### Lightspeed (Lightspeed Commerce / Lightspeed Restaurant)

- **What it is / category** — Cloud POS and commerce platform for retail **and** hospitality/restaurants; multi-vertical, heavily built via acquisition. Strong internationally.

- **Founders & backgrounds** — Dax Dasilva, a computer-science-trained entrepreneur (dropped out of UBC, later completed the degree). Motivation: give small independent businesses software as good as what big chains had (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dax_Dasilva; en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightspeed_Commerce). Retail/software roots, not restaurant operations.

- **Founding story** — Founded September 1, 2005, in Montreal, Quebec. Initial product was elegant, user-friendly **Mac desktop POS software** for small independent retailers overlooked by legacy vendors (source: en.wikipedia.org; pestel-analysis.com). Restaurants/hospitality came later as a deliberate vertical expansion, largely through M&A.

- **Funding history** — ~$750K seed (2005) from friends/family; then **bootstrapped ~7 years**. Series A: **~$30M from Accel (2012)** — the capital that funded the pivot from Mac desktop software to cloud POS. ~$35M (Sept 2014, iNovia + Accel). **Series C ~$61M (Sept 2015)** led by Caisse de dépôt et placement du Québec and Investissement Québec (source: pestel-analysis.com; ivey.uwo.ca). **IPO: March 2019 on the Toronto Stock Exchange (~$1.7B valuation)** — described as the biggest Canadian tech IPO in ~a decade — then **dual-listed on NYSE in September 2020**. Current status: **public (TSX/NYSE: LSPD)**. Dasilva was CEO ~16 years, stepped down Feb 2022, reappointed CEO in 2024.

- **First customers & early GTM (MOST IMPORTANT)** — Original wedge was **beautifully designed, affordable POS for independent Mac-using retailers** — an underserved, design-conscious niche; sold via a mix of channel/partners and inbound plus the Apple/Mac ecosystem halo. For restaurants specifically, Lightspeed's GTM was **acquire-and-integrate**: it bought regional hospitality POS leaders and folded their customer bases and products into a unified platform — Australia's **Kounta** (2019, ~7,000 locations in ANZ), Switzerland's **iKentoo**, Germany's **Gastrofix**, and US-based **Upserve** (2020, ~$430M, adding ~7,000 US restaurant locations and >$6B TTM GTV) (source: prnewswire.com; lightspeedhq.com; betakit.com). It then launched a **unified flagship Lightspeed Restaurant** platform in North America (2021–2022).

- **Evolution & pivots** — Mac desktop → cloud POS (2012, Accel-funded) → multi-vertical (retail + hospitality) → **M&A roll-up** (Kounta, iKentoo, Gastrofix, Upserve, ShopKeep, Vend, Ecwid) → unified Lightspeed platform + Lightspeed Payments. FY2023 showed continued revenue gains (source: betakit.com). Positions as a global, omnichannel commerce platform rather than a US-only restaurant specialist.

- **Restaurant-specific lessons**
  - **You can buy your way into the restaurant vertical** — acquiring regional hospitality POS players buys installed base, local product depth, and payments attach. But integration risk is real (multiple codebases to unify).
  - **Design and ease-of-use are a legitimate wedge** for independents who feel ignored by clunky legacy systems.
  - **Payments attach is the monetization flywheel** just as with Toast/Square — buy locations, then convert them to Lightspeed Payments.
  - International/independent focus differentiates from US-centric rivals, but the restaurant business is only part of a broader retail story — **less restaurant-specialized than Toast**.

- **Sources** — en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightspeed_Commerce; en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dax_Dasilva; pestel-analysis.com; ivey.uwo.ca; prnewswire.com (Kounta, Upserve); lightspeedhq.com; betakit.com.

### SpotOn

- **What it is / category** — Restaurant-focused payments + POS + software platform for SMBs (POS, online ordering, marketing, loyalty, reservations, labor). A direct Toast/Square challenger.

- **Founders & backgrounds** — Twin brothers **Matt Hyman and Zach Hyman** (Co-founders, Co-CEOs) — each with 20+ years in **payment processing**; they previously built and sold **Central Payment (CPAY)** to Total System Services (TSYS) at a reported ~$840M valuation — plus Chicago fintech entrepreneur **Doron Friedman** (Co-founder, Chief Innovation Officer). Deep payments-industry DNA, not restaurant operators (source: spoton.com/blog; businesswire.com; tracxn.com).

- **Founding story** — Founded 2017. Thesis: SMBs are ~99.7% of US businesses yet are underserved by fintech; build an integrated software+payments platform with human, local support. Restaurants became the strategic center of gravity over time (source: prnewswire.com; spoton.com).

- **Funding history** — Series A 2018: **$20M self-funded by the Hyman founders** (source: spoton.com/blog; prnewswire.com "Founders Commit $20 Million"). Series B 2019: **$40M** led by Franklin Templeton and Dragoneer. Series D May 2021: **$125M led by Andreessen Horowitz (a16z)**, ~$1.875B valuation. Series E 2021: **$300M**, ~$3.15B. **Series F May 2022: $300M at a $3.6B valuation** (after reported ~100% ARR growth in 2021) (source: techcrunch.com; businesswire.com; crunchbase.com). Total raised **~$920M+** (source: tracxn.com). **October 2023 layoffs** occurred as growth-stage fintech valuations compressed (source: trueup.io; glassdoor.com). Current status: **private**; last headline valuation **$3.6B (2022)** — note that a subsequent down-round or markdown was widely rumored but we **could not confirm a specific revised figure** from primary sources; treat $3.6B as the last confirmed number.

- **First customers & early GTM (MOST IMPORTANT)** — Wedge: **integrated payments + free/low-cost software + relentless local human support**, explicitly positioned against faceless processors and against Toast/Square. The GTM signature is a **large local field sales force plus in-person implementation, training, and dedicated local reps + 24/7 support** — relationship selling. SpotOn markets that customers "talk to my reps like they're my friends," and bundles online ordering, automated marketing, AI profit analysis and reservations into one monthly package (source: spoton.com; usfoods.com; capterra.com). Distribution partnership with **US Foods** extended reach into restaurant accounts. The founders' payments background meant they could **subsidize software with payment economics** from day one.

- **Evolution & pivots** — Broad SMB payments/software → increasingly **restaurant-first** POS platform; acquisitions (e.g., Appetize enterprise/stadium POS in 2021; Emagine/others) pushed it up-market into enterprise and sports/entertainment venues. Post-2022 it refocused on efficiency and profitability after the funding-market reset and 2023 layoffs.

- **Restaurant-specific lessons**
  - **"Local rep + human support" is a durable wedge against remote/self-serve competitors** — restaurants value a person who shows up, especially at the independent end.
  - **Founders with payments economics can undercut on software price** and still make money on the processing spread — a structural advantage in restaurant tech.
  - **Channel/distribution partnerships (US Foods)** put you in front of restaurant owners you'd never cold-call efficiently.
  - Caution: growth funded by cheap capital and heavy field-sales headcount is **expensive to sustain** — the 2023 layoffs show the model's sensitivity to the funding cycle.

- **Sources** — spoton.com/blog; prnewswire.com; businesswire.com; techcrunch.com (2022 $300M/$3.6B); tracxn.com; trueup.io; glassdoor.com; usfoods.com.

### TouchBistro

- **What it is / category** — iPad-based POS built specifically for restaurants (front-of-house first), plus reservations, online ordering, loyalty, marketing, payments. One of the earliest iPad restaurant POS pioneers.

- **Founders & backgrounds** — **Alex Barrotti**, a serial entrepreneur who had earlier founded **INEX** (a web storefront builder) sold to Infospace (later Blucora) for ~$45M in 1999. Software/e-commerce background, not a restaurateur — but the idea came directly from a restaurant-owner friend's pain (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TouchBistro_Inc.; ediblebrooklyn.com).

- **Founding story** — Conceived 2010; app launched 2011. Barrotti was eating sushi on a Caribbean patio where the owner (friend Yoshi Ono) asked him to find technology to fix operational chaos — waiters running in and out to take orders, deliver food, and take payment, killing table turns. When the **iPad launched (2010)**, Barrotti saw the perfect tableside form factor, found **no restaurant POS apps existed for it**, assembled a team, and shipped one of the first robust iPad restaurant POS apps in 2011 (source: ediblebrooklyn.com; en.wikipedia.org). Distinctive early choice: **mobile, tableside, waiter-centric** design.

- **Funding history** — Raised CDN funding across rounds; total **~US$275M / CDN$300M+** to date. Headline round: **Series E September 18, 2019 — CDN$158M (~US$118.8M) led by OMERS Growth Equity**, with participation from **Barclays Bank, RBC Ventures, BMO Capital Partners, JPMorgan Chase, Napier Park, BDC, Kensington Capital** and existing investors (source: businesswire.com; touchbistro.com press; omers.com; fintechfutures.com). Current status: **private**. (Note: an OpenTable strategic tie/investment is sometimes cited; we **could not confirm OpenTable as an investor** in the Series E from primary sources — the confirmed lead was OMERS. Treat OpenTable involvement as unverified.)

- **First customers & early GTM (MOST IMPORTANT)** — Wedge: an **affordable, easy, iPad-native POS** for small/independent full-service restaurants at a time when the alternative was expensive, clunky legacy terminals (Micros/Aloha) costing tens of thousands. Distribution leaned heavily on the **Apple App Store** as a discovery/download channel — restaurateurs could find and try it directly — plus inbound and a direct sales team; global reach came early (the product sold internationally via the App Store). The "yes" was **low upfront cost, mobility (order at the table), and no need for a big installed server system.** Famous positioning: built by/for restaurants, "the POS that works the way restaurants work."

- **Evolution & pivots** — Single iPad POS app → full platform: reservations, online ordering, loyalty, gift cards, marketing, and **integrated payments (TouchBistro Payments)**. Expanded via acquisitions (e.g., reservations/marketing tools). Offices in Toronto, NYC, Chicago, LA, London, Mexico City. Competes with Toast/Square at the SMB full-service end, especially internationally.

- **Restaurant-specific lessons**
  - **Form factor can be the wedge**: the iPad let a scrappy entrant leapfrog legacy terminals and sell "tableside mobility" restaurants instantly understood.
  - **The App Store lowered CAC** — restaurants could self-discover and download, unusual in a field-sales-dominated category.
  - **Low upfront cost beats capex** for independents wary of $20K+ legacy POS installs.
  - Being **early but not owning payments/processing at first** left monetization on the table versus Toast — later corrected with TouchBistro Payments. Owning payments early matters.

- **Sources** — en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TouchBistro_Inc.; ediblebrooklyn.com; businesswire.com (Series E); touchbistro.com press releases; omers.com; fintechfutures.com; crunchbase.com.

### Clover (Clover Network / Fiserv)

- **What it is / category** — Cloud POS hardware + software platform (Station, Mini, Flex, Go) for SMBs including restaurants and retail. Distributed at massive scale through banks/ISOs; owned by **Fiserv**.

- **Founders & backgrounds** — Founded by **John Beatty, Leonard Speiser, Mark Schulze, and Kelvin Zheng** — Silicon Valley software/product entrepreneurs. Speiser had prior startup experience (e.g., Bix, and earlier eBay/LinkedIn-adjacent roles); the team applied a "modern app platform" mindset to merchant hardware (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clover_Network; golden.com; medium.com/speroventures).

- **Founding story** — Founded October 2010, Silicon Valley (Mountain View). Insight (partly Uber-inspired): payments and merchant point-of-sale were ripe to be reinvented as an **open app platform on modern hardware** — a POS you could extend with third-party apps like a smartphone. Raised **$5.5M seed (Nov 2010, Sutter Hill Ventures lead, with a16z and angels)**. First hardware, the **Clover Station**, launched 2013 and shipped January 2014 (source: en.wikipedia.org; techcrunch.com; sec.gov 8-K).

- **Funding history** — Seed $5.5M (2010). **Acquired by First Data in December 2012 for ~$56.1M** — very early, pre-major-revenue (source: en.wikipedia.org; techcrunch.com). Then scaled inside First Data's enormous bank/ISO distribution. **Fiserv acquired First Data in January/July 2019** (~$22B deal). Current status: **a division of Fiserv (NYSE: FI)**. Scale: **~700,000 businesses in ~8 countries**; **annualized GPV ~$313B (mid-2024), Clover revenue ~$2.7B in 2024 (+~29%)** — larger payment volume than Square or Toast (source: pymnts.com; digitaltransactions.net; paymentsdive.com; finance.yahoo.com).

- **First customers & early GTM (MOST IMPORTANT)** — Clover's defining GTM is **distribution through banks, merchant-acquirers, and ISOs (independent sales organizations)** — not direct field sales to restaurants. After the First Data acquisition, Clover rode First Data's/Fiserv's vast network of **bank partners and reseller sales reps** who already had merchant relationships and could bundle Clover hardware with a merchant processing account. The wedge for the merchant: **an approachable, app-extensible POS you get through your bank/payment processor**, often with the hardware financed and payments pre-attached. This channel model is why Clover reached enormous GPV without Toast-style boots-on-the-ground — the "yes" often came from a trusted banker/ISO rep, not Clover itself. Restaurants are one segment among many (retail, services).

- **Evolution & pivots** — App-platform POS → acquired early by First Data → scaled via bank/ISO channel → deepened restaurant/vertical software (Clover for Restaurants, online ordering, Clover Dining) to compete with Toast. Now Fiserv's flagship SMB growth engine.

- **Restaurant-specific lessons**
  - **Channel/ISO distribution can out-scale direct field sales on payment volume** — leveraging banks' existing merchant relationships is a fundamentally different, capital-light path to reach.
  - Trade-off: channel-sold POS tends to be **less restaurant-specialized and lower-touch** than Toast/SpotOn; deep full-service features and hospitality support are weaker, so Clover skews to quick-service, retail, and simpler operations.
  - **Selling through the entity that already owns the merchant's payments (the bank/processor) removes the payments-attach fight** — payments come pre-bundled.
  - An **early acquisition (at ~$56M) traded founder upside for distribution** — the platform only reached hundreds of billions in GPV because of First Data's channel.

- **Sources** — en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clover_Network; golden.com; techcrunch.com (First Data acquisition); sec.gov First Data 8-K; medium.com/speroventures; pymnts.com; digitaltransactions.net; paymentsdive.com.

### Revel Systems

- **What it is / category** — iPad-based POS for restaurants and retail (also kiosks); an early iPad-POS pioneer. Now owned by **Shift4**. A cautionary tale of founder ouster and stalled momentum.

- **Founders & backgrounds** — **Lisa Falzone** (CEO; ex-competitive swimmer, Stanford grad, sales/business) and **Christopher Ciabarra** (CTO; security/engineering). Not restaurant operators; a business+engineering pairing (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revel_Systems; en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisa_Falzone).

- **Founding story** — Founded 2010, San Francisco. Bet early on the **iPad as a POS platform** (same insight window as TouchBistro), building an iOS-based cloud POS for restaurants and quick-service. Falzone's scrappy fundraising is legendary — she reportedly saved the startup early by convincing "a random guy in a coffee shop" to invest ~$28,000 (source: finance.yahoo.com). First institutional money: **$3.7M from DCM (May 2011)** (source: en.wikipedia.org).

- **Funding history** — $3.7M (DCM, 2011). Large **Series C ~$110M total** — **Welsh, Carson, Anderson & Stowe (WCAS) led Series C-1 (Nov 2014)**, **Intuit led Series C-2 (Dec 2014)**, plus ~$13.5M from ROTH Capital (2015) (source: en.wikipedia.org). Peaked around a **~$500M valuation**. Founders **ousted**: majority investor **WCAS removed Falzone and Ciabarra** — Falzone replaced as CEO in **February 2017**. Current status: **acquired by Shift4 in June 2024** (price not consistently disclosed; treat as undisclosed) (source: en.wikipedia.org).

- **First customers & early GTM (MOST IMPORTANT)** — Wedge: **affordable iPad POS with strong QSR/chain features and kiosk/self-order** capability, versus expensive legacy terminals. Notable early GTM angle: **App Store presence + landing recognizable chain names** as reference customers — Revel publicized clients like **Cinnabon, Popeyes, Little Caesars, and Twistee Treat**, and in March 2012 launched **"Kiosk Mode," billed as the world's first consumer-facing iPad POS kiosk** (self-order/self-pay), which it rolled out at Twistee Treat (source: prnewswire.com; en.wikipedia.org; qsrmagazine.com; twisteetreat.com case study). Chasing **multi-unit chains** gave big logo credibility and volume but also complex, demanding deployments.

- **Evolution & pivots** — iPad POS → pushed up-market into **enterprise/multi-unit chains and grocery/retail** and self-service kiosks → founder removal (2017) and slowing momentum amid intense competition from Toast/Square → **acquired by Shift4 (2024)** as a payments-attach play. A story of early lead squandered.

- **Restaurant-specific lessons**
  - **Being early to a form factor (iPad) is necessary but not sufficient** — Toast and Square out-executed on payments, support, and go-to-market.
  - **Kiosk/self-order was a genuinely prescient bet** (well ahead of the QSR kiosk wave) — read the labor/throughput trends early.
  - **Landing big chain logos is double-edged**: great for credibility, but enterprise deployments are heavy and can distract from a scalable SMB motion.
  - **Governance matters**: taking large PE/VC control money (WCAS majority) cost the founders the company — cautionary on cap-table control in a capital-intensive category.

- **Sources** — en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revel_Systems; en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisa_Falzone; finance.yahoo.com; prnewswire.com (Cinnabon, Kiosk Mode); qsrmagazine.com; twisteetreat.com case study.

### Olo

- **What it is / category** — B2B, "invisible" digital-ordering and commerce platform for **enterprise multi-location restaurant brands** (online/mobile ordering, delivery dispatch, payments, guest data). SaaS + transaction model. Not a POS; the ordering/data layer.

- **Founders & backgrounds** — **Noah Glass**, founder/CEO. Background in finance/tech (ex-Endeavor; studied at Yale/Harvard). Not a restaurateur; an early-mobile-commerce visionary who bet on ordering-ahead a decade before it was mainstream (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olo_(online_ordering); nrn.com; aws.amazon.com).

- **Founding story** — Founded 2005 in New York as **GoMobo**. First product: **order-ahead via SMS/text** — pre-order coffee/food for pickup and skip the line (radically early — pre-iPhone). Over time Glass realized the durable business was **B2B infrastructure**, not a consumer brand, and in **2010 rebranded to Olo ("online ordering")** — becoming the invisible engine restaurants use to run their own digital ordering rather than a consumer-facing app (source: en.wikipedia.org; grokipedia.com). This "go invisible / power the brands" pivot is the crux of Olo's story.

- **Funding history** — VC-backed over many years (investors included Shasta Ventures, PayPal/Peter Thiel-linked capital, Tiger Global, and others; specific early round sizes not fully enumerated here — treat as not-fully-verified). **IPO: March 17, 2021, on NYSE (ticker OLO), raising ~$450M at roughly a $3.6B valuation** (source: en.wikipedia.org; nrn.com). Current status: **public (NYSE: OLO)**; FY2024 revenue roughly **~$240M** with a hybrid subscription + transaction model (~50–55% subscription; growing transaction/payments share) (source: alexandre.substack.com; matrixbcg.com — treat revenue split as approximate). Note: In 2025 there was press about a take-private/PE transaction — we did **not** confirm details here, so treat current status as public pending verification.

- **First customers & early GTM (MOST IMPORTANT)** — The wedge: **be the ordering infrastructure for big chains, not a competing consumer marketplace.** Olo's GTM is **direct enterprise sales to multi-location brands** — long, relationship-driven deals with brands like **Shake Shack, Denny's, Wingstop, Five Guys, Cosi** and hundreds of others. The pitch that made brands say yes: **"we're invisible — you keep your brand, your app, your guest relationship and data; we power the plumbing and integrate with your POS."** That neutrality (not competing with the restaurant's brand, unlike DoorDash/Grubhub marketplaces) is the core selling proposition. Land one enterprise logo → expand across all its locations (land-and-expand at the brand level, not store-by-store). Early famous product: Olo built **Shake Shack's "Shake App" (2017)**.

- **Evolution & pivots** — Consumer GoMobo (SMS order-ahead) → invisible B2B Olo (2010) → **Order + Dispatch + Pay + Engage** platform suites → acquisitions (**Wisely** customer intelligence, **Omnivore** POS integration middleware) and **Olo Pay** (embedded payments) to expand revenue per brand. Scale: powers ordering for a large share of the top US enterprise restaurant brands and hundreds of millions of orders.

- **Restaurant-specific lessons**
  - **"Invisible infrastructure" beats "competing consumer brand"** when selling to established restaurant brands — never threaten the operator's guest relationship or data.
  - **Enterprise land-and-expand**: one brand HQ decision rolls out across hundreds/thousands of locations — very different (and higher-leverage) than SMB store-by-store selling.
  - **Neutrality is a moat**: by integrating with everyone's POS and every delivery channel, Olo becomes the switchboard restaurants trust.
  - **Timing/patience**: being ~5+ years too early (SMS order-ahead in 2005) required a pivot; the winning model (B2B) emerged only after the consumer thesis failed.

- **Sources** — en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olo_(online_ordering); nrn.com; aws.amazon.com/blogs/industries; grokipedia.com; alexandre.substack.com; matrixbcg.com; pymnts.com.

### ChowNow

- **What it is / category** — Commission-free **direct online ordering** platform for independent restaurants — branded websites/apps, marketplace, marketing — on a flat SaaS subscription (the anti-Grubhub).

- **Founders & backgrounds** — **Christopher (Chris) Webb** (CEO) — finance background: internships/roles at **Oppenheimer, Bear Stearns, Lehman Brothers** — and **Eric Jaffe** — a former **commercial real estate investor**. Neither a restaurateur, but Webb had direct exposure to restaurant economics via his mother's investment in the **Tender Greens** restaurant chain, where he learned firsthand the pain of third-party delivery fees (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ChowNow; dot.la; ventureforamerica.org).

- **Founding story** — Founded 2011 (Los Angeles); platform launched March 2012. Origin insight: independent restaurants were being **gouged by high-commission marketplaces (Grubhub/Seamless)** and had no way to take **direct, branded, commission-free** orders. The mission crystallized as "help local restaurants avoid onerous marketplace fees and own their customer relationship." To validate demand, Webb and Jaffe **built a fake/mock product and drove to Santa Barbara to see if restaurateurs would buy it** — classic pre-build customer validation (source: dot.la; techcrunch.com podcast).

- **Funding history** — Seed ~$1M (2013) from **GRP Partners (now Upfront Ventures)**, James Bailey, angels; plus **Launchpad LA** accelerator; ~$4M+ total raised by Jan 2013. Later ~$10M round led by **Upfront Ventures**; multiple subsequent rounds. Total raised is in the **low hundreds of millions** across its history (exact cumulative figure not fully verified here). Current status: **private**, still independent (source: en.wikipedia.org; crunchbase.com; bonfirevc.com). (We did not find a confirmed recent valuation — treat as unverified.)

- **First customers & early GTM (MOST IMPORTANT)** — The wedge and the entire pitch is **economic alignment: flat monthly subscription, 0% commission on direct orders** (vs. Grubhub/DoorDash taking 15–30%). Pricing today runs roughly **$119–$328/month tiers + payment processing (~2.95% + $0.29) + setup fees** — but the constant is **predictable flat fee, not per-order commission** (source: get.chownow.com/pricing; getsauce.com). Early GTM: **direct sales to independent restaurants** with a message operators immediately grasp ("stop paying 20–30% to the marketplaces; own your customers and your app"). ChowNow builds each restaurant a **branded ordering website + custom-branded mobile app**, so the restaurant keeps its identity and customer data. Most restaurants go live within a few weeks. The "yes" is pure math: **keep more of every order + own the guest relationship.**

- **Evolution & pivots** — Consumer-ish local ordering idea → **B2B SaaS for restaurants** (branded direct ordering) → added a discovery **marketplace**, loyalty/CRM, and more affordable delivery options; deliberately stayed **flat-fee/commission-free** as its brand identity. Positioned as the mission-driven, restaurant-friendly alternative through the delivery wars.

- **Restaurant-specific lessons**
  - **Attack the incumbent's pricing model, not just its features**: "flat fee, 0% commission" is a one-sentence value prop restaurateurs viscerally understand and want.
  - **"Own your customers and your brand"** resonates deeply with independents who resent renting their customer relationship from marketplaces.
  - **Pre-build validation (the fake product / Santa Barbara test)** de-risked the concept before writing code — a cheap way to confirm restaurants will pay.
  - **Founder credibility via proximity** (Webb's family Tender Greens exposure) substituted for not being a restaurateur — enough to understand the real pain.
  - Trade-off: flat-fee model means **slower revenue-per-account growth** than payment-take-rate models; harder to fund massive field sales, so growth is steadier/leaner.

- **Sources** — en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ChowNow; dot.la; ventureforamerica.org; techcrunch.com (founder podcast); get.chownow.com/pricing; getsauce.com; crunchbase.com; bonfirevc.com.

### Owner.com

- **What it is / category** — All-in-one **digital marketing + commission-free online ordering** SaaS for independent ("mom-and-pop") restaurants: websites, branded apps, SEO, CRM, loyalty, marketing automation, direct ordering. The newest, fastest-rising challenger in this batch.

- **Founders & backgrounds** — **Adam Guild** (Co-founder/CEO) — a **high-school dropout** who taught himself local SEO as a teenager; and **Dean Bloembergen** (Co-founder/CTO, joined early 2021) who built technology for large restaurant chains including **Blaze Pizza**. Guild is emphatically **not** from restaurants or finance — his "school" was rescuing his mother's failing business (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Owner.com; en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Guild; inc.com; fortune.com; forbes.com).

- **Founding story** — Guild started the company at **age 17 in 2018** (dropping out of high school). The origin insight came from applying **local SEO** to save his single mother's failing **dog-grooming salon in West Hollywood** — optimizing for queries like "dog grooming West Hollywood," which flooded the salon with Google-sourced customers and turned it around within a year. He realized independent restaurants had the identical problem (invisible online, dependent on third parties) and pivoted the business to serve them. **Renamed to Owner.com in 2021** when Bloembergen joined (source: inc.com; review.firstround.com; owner.com/our-story).

- **Funding history** — Seed ~$10.7M (led by **SaaStr Fund**); **Series A $15M (March 2022, led by Altman Capital)**; **Series B $33M (January 2024)** — **Redpoint Ventures** and Altman Capital, with Horsley Bridge, Activant, Transpose; **Series C $120M (May 2025)** led by **Meritech Capital Partners and Headline**, valuing Owner.com at **~$1B (unicorn)**. Total raised **~$189M** (source: restaurantbusinessonline.com; techcrunch.com; tracxn.com; fortune.com). Current status: **private, ~$1B last valuation (2025)**. Notable: Guild attracted marquee angels/backers despite being a young dropout.

- **First customers & early GTM (MOST IMPORTANT)** — This is the richest wedge story in the batch. The **first restaurant customer** came from **brute-force, personalized cold outreach**: Guild **emailed Morten Kaag, owner of Tortilla Republic (West Hollywood), seven times, then cold-texted him.** In the demo, Guild showed what he'd done for his mom's dog-grooming business — and Kaag realized **he personally was a Google-sourced customer of that exact salon** ("that's where we take our dogs... I found it on Google"). Guild's line: **"We'll do the same for your restaurant."** Kaag balked at the **$2,000/month** price but agreed to a **90-day, results-based trial** — if Guild could meaningfully lift sales. 90 days later Tortilla Republic's sales were up **>$10,000/month** (source: review.firstround.com; inc.com; grokipedia.com). The GTM DNA: **outcome-based selling (drive measurable incremental sales), a concrete before/after proof, relentless founder persistence, and commission-free direct ordering** so the restaurant keeps its margin. Big post-COVID pivot: leaned into **helping restaurants own their online presence and direct orders** as delivery-marketplace fatigue peaked.

- **Evolution & pivots** — WordPress-based SMB website builder → restaurant-specific SEO/marketing → **all-in-one commission-free ordering + marketing platform** for independents. Post-COVID pivot (2020–2021) sharply focused it on restaurants and direct digital revenue; explosive funding trajectory 2022→2025 to unicorn status.

- **Restaurant-specific lessons**
  - **Sell outcomes, not software**: "I'll grow your sales, measurably, in 90 days" (with a proof story) beats feature lists — restaurateurs care about revenue, not tech.
  - **Results-based / trial pricing lowers the risk of the yes** for skeptical independent owners; back yourself to deliver.
  - **A vivid, personal proof point (the dog-grooming turnaround)** is more persuasive than credentials — especially when the prospect *is* one of your proof's customers.
  - **Relentless, personalized founder outreach (7 emails + a text)** works in a low-trust, hard-to-reach buyer base — persistence signals you'll also show up for support.
  - **Commission-free + "own your customers"** (same as ChowNow) is the durable positioning against marketplaces.

### Slice

- **What it is / category** — Vertically-focused ordering + marketing + tech platform **exclusively for independent pizzerias** — helping mom-and-pop pizza shops compete with Domino's/Pizza Hut. A "reverse franchise" arming independents with chain-grade tech.

- **Founders & backgrounds** — **Ilir Sela** (founder/CEO), an **Albanian immigrant whose extended family owns numerous pizzerias** — genuine, deep domain roots (rare in this batch). Prior company: **Nerd Force**, a web-design/IT-services firm he launched in 2003 and **sold for ~$500K in 2008**, which funded Slice's start (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slice_(app); forbes.com; research.contrary.com; ninaroberts.net).

- **Founding story** — Founded 2010 as **MyPizza**, rebranded **Slice in 2015**. Insight born from the family business: independent pizzerias were losing to chains purely on **technology/online ordering**, not on product. Sela set out to give the ~ tens of thousands of US independent pizzerias a **mobile-optimized website + app + ordering** so they could compete digitally — a hyper-focused single-vertical bet (source: forbes.com; en.wikipedia.org). His **first customers were literally family and friends** — his brother-in-law, cousins, and friends who owned pizzerias.

- **Funding history** — **Bootstrapped for years** using the Nerd Force proceeds — famously "scaled the business going door to door without raising a dime" initially, reaching **~2,000 shops and ~$40M GMV before institutional VC** (source: linkedin.com/Nichole Wischoff; research.contrary.com). Then: **Series A ~$3M (July 2016)**; **~$15M (May 2017) led by GGV Capital**; later rounds; **Series C $43M (May 2020) led by KKR**. Total raised **~$82M+** (some sources cite $100M+ across all rounds) (source: en.wikipedia.org; medium.com/Primary; alejandrocremades.com). Current status: **private**, valued **~$1B+ enterprise value** (per founder interviews; treat as approximate) (source: newsletter.lukesophinos.com).

- **First customers & early GTM (MOST IMPORTANT)** — The purest **founder-led, door-to-door, guerrilla** GTM in the batch. Sela started with **his own family's and friends' pizzerias**, then literally **walked door-to-door to sign shops**. Famous growth hack: he **wrapped advertising banners around a car and parked it in front of target pizzerias** to get their attention — near-zero-cost guerrilla marketing that grew MyPizza to **80+ NYC pizzerias** early, then thousands. The wedge/pitch: **"compete with Domino's for a flat, fair per-order fee"** — Slice charges pizzerias a **low flat fee per order (famously ~$1.85–$2.25 per order, far below marketplace commissions)** rather than a percentage, and handles the tech, marketing, and even supplies. The "yes": **cheaper than Grubhub, purpose-built for pizza, and run by someone who understands pizzerias.** Deep vertical focus (only pizza) let Slice tailor everything (menus, toppings logic, marketing) to one cuisine.

- **Evolution & pivots** — MyPizza (2010) → Slice (2015) → expanded from ordering into a **full pizzeria operating stack**: Slice POS/register, Slice Ovens (equipment/supplies via group buying — "Slice Accelerate"), branded sites/apps, marketing, and data — an all-in-one for independents. Scale: **~20,000 pizzerias across all 50 states**, surpassed **$1B+ GMV**, ~700+ employees (source: blog.slicelife.com; slice.com/our-story; foundationinc.co).

- **Restaurant-specific lessons**
  - **Hyper-vertical focus wins**: owning *one* cuisine (pizza) lets you build exactly the right product, marketing, and even supply chain — and become the trusted default for that niche.
  - **Domain authenticity sells**: a founder from a pizzeria family earns instant trust that outsiders spend years buying — huge in a relationship-driven, skeptical buyer base.
  - **Flat per-order fee vs. commission** is the same margin-alignment wedge as ChowNow/Owner, tuned for pizza economics.
  - **Guerrilla, near-zero-CAC founder selling** (door-to-door, the banner-wrapped car, starting with friends/family) proved the model before any VC — capital-efficient and credible.
  - **Expand from ordering into the whole operation** (POS, supplies, ovens) once you own the relationship — land on ordering, expand into everything a pizzeria buys.

## Cross-cutting observations (this batch)

1. **Two dominant first-customer motions — and both work, at different ends of the market.** (a) **Founder-led, in-person, door-to-door field sales** for independents/SMBs (Toast, SpotOn, Slice, Owner, early TouchBistro) — the high cost-of-sale is a moat because it scares off lighter-touch competitors; and (b) **channel/enterprise distribution** — banks/ISOs (Clover), enterprise brand HQ sales (Olo), or App Store/product-led self-serve (Square, early TouchBistro). The middle rarely wins. Restaurants are a high-trust, in-person buyer; if you sell to independents, expect to show up physically.

2. **Restaurants buy outcomes and revenue, not software.** The pitches that closed were always operational/financial: *turn tables faster* (Toast), *grow your sales in 90 days* (Owner's results-based trial), *stop paying 20–30% to marketplaces* (ChowNow/Slice/Owner), *compete with Domino's* (Slice). Feature lists don't move restaurateurs; before/after proof and margin math do.

3. **The pricing wedge is often the product.** Attacking the incumbent's *business model* — flat fee vs. commission (ChowNow, Slice, Owner), free reader/no monthly (Square), cheaper than legacy terminals (TouchBistro/Revel) — is a one-sentence value prop owners instantly grasp. "Commission-free" and "own your customers/data" are recurring, emotionally resonant hooks against the delivery marketplaces.

4. **Payments is the monetization engine; software is the wedge.** The biggest winners (Toast, Square, Clover, SpotOn, Lightspeed) land the account on software/hardware and make the real money on the **payment take-rate**, which scales automatically with the restaurant's volume (aligned incentives). Pure flat-fee SaaS players (ChowNow) grow revenue-per-account more slowly — a structural constraint on how much field sales they can fund. Owning payments early is a repeated lesson (TouchBistro/Revel monetized payments late and left money on the table).

5. **24/7, local, human support is a sales weapon, not a cost center.** Toast's "24/7 support," SpotOn's "local reps who feel like friends," Owner's persistence — restaurants run nights/weekends and can't tolerate downtime, so support and relationships close deals and reduce churn.

6. **Outsiders win by embedding; insiders win by authenticity.** Most founders were NOT restaurateurs (Toast/Endeca engineers, Square's Dorsey/McKelvey, Olo's Glass, ChowNow's finance guys, Owner's teenage SEO hacker) — they compensated by **shadowing operators and solving a proven pain**. The exceptions (Slice's pizzeria family, and proximity cases like ChowNow's Tender Greens link) show domain authenticity is a genuine accelerant of trust in this skeptical buyer base.

7. **The pivot is often the whole story.** The category-definers pivoted hard: Toast (consumer app → own the POS), Olo (consumer SMS ordering → invisible B2B infrastructure), Owner (dog-grooming/SMB SEO → restaurants), Square/Lightspeed (horizontal → restaurant-specific vertical). Restaurants are hard enough that the first idea usually isn't the winning one; the insight is real but the wrapper changes.

8. **Vertical specialization compounds.** The clearest, most capital-efficient GTM stories are the hyper-focused ones (Slice = only pizza; Owner/ChowNow = only independent restaurants; Olo = only enterprise brands). Focus lets you tailor product, marketing, pricing, and even supply chain, and become the default for a niche. Horizontal players (Square, Clover, Lightspeed) had to bolt on restaurant depth later and remain comparatively shallow in full-service hospitality.

9. **Land-and-expand operates at two altitudes.** SMB players expand *within an account* (POS → payments → payroll → lending → marketing: Toast, Slice into POS/ovens/supplies). Enterprise players (Olo) expand *across an account's locations* — one HQ decision rolls out to hundreds/thousands of stores. Know which altitude you're selling at.

10. **Capital structure and timing are existential in this category.** Revel lost its founders to a controlling PE investor; SpotOn's field-sales-heavy, cheap-capital growth forced 2023 layoffs when the market turned; Clover traded founder upside for First Data's distribution. The restaurant-tech land grab is expensive (in-person sales, hardware, support), so how you fund the GTM — and who controls the cap table — shapes the outcome as much as the product.
