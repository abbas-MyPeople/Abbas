# PART A — BACK-OFFICE / INVENTORY / ACCOUNTING

## Restaurant365

- **What it is / category.** Cloud-native, all-in-one restaurant enterprise-management platform ("restaurant-specific ERP"): accounting + back-office operations + inventory + scheduling/labor + (later) payroll/HR and banking. The enterprise-heaviest of the U.S. "independents-to-emerging-chains" players. HQ: Irvine, CA (source: restaurant365.com).

- **Founders & backgrounds.** Co-founded by **Tony Smith** (CEO; BS business management + info systems, BYU), **John Moody** (Chief Strategist), and **Morgan Harris** (Chief Community Officer; CPA who trained at PwC). All three previously worked together at **Dynamic Methods**, a Microsoft Dynamics/Great Plains value-added reseller that sold accounting+operations software to 300+ companies across many industries. Crucially, the founders were **software/accounting people, not restaurant operators** (source: restaurant365.com; hospitalitytech.com; businessmodelcanvastemplate.com).

- **Founding story.** Founded **2011**. At Dynamic Methods the trio saw that connecting accounting to operations drove business success, but generic ERP required disjointed bolt-ons. As software moved to the cloud in 2011 they decided to pick ONE industry and go deep; they found no fully cloud-based, fully connected accounting+operations product existed for restaurants. To overcome their lack of domain pedigree they interviewed restaurateurs after closing time and counted inventory alongside managers. An oft-cited early proof point: their **fourth customer** (their largest at the time) reported after ~1 year that it had **doubled in size without adding corporate/back-office staff** — validating the ROI thesis (source: restaurant365.com/blog; content.calibbq.media).

- **Funding history.** **Bootstrapped ~7 years** (≈20 employees by 2014, ≈100 by 2018) before institutional capital.
  - **Series A — ~$20M, 2018, led by Bessemer Venture Partners.** (Some sources describe A+B together, Jan 2018 + Jan 2019, both Bessemer-led, as "nearly $40M.") (source: restaurant365.com/press; prnewswire.com)
  - **Series C — $88M, June 2019, led by ICONIQ Capital** (ICONIQ's first investment). (source: prnewswire.com)
  - **Series D — $135M, May 2023, co-led by KKR and L Catterton** (ICONIQ Growth + Bessemer participating). Disclosed it had **passed a $1B valuation** (unicorn). (source: restaurant365.com; techcrunch.com)
  - **Series E — $175M, May 2024, led by ICONIQ Growth** (KKR + L Catterton participating), at a **$1B+ valuation** (undisclosed exact figure). (source: techcrunch.com; finance.yahoo.com; prnewswire.com)
  - **Total raised: reported inconsistently.** Summing disclosed rounds (~$20M + ~$20M + $88M + $135M + $175M) ≈ **~$438M**; Crunchbase cites "$283M over 4 rounds" (appears to exclude early Bessemer rounds); Tracxn cites up to "$445M over 7 rounds." Best estimate **~$430–445M total**, but flag the aggregator disagreement. **Status: private, growth/PE-backed** (ICONIQ, KKR, L Catterton, Bessemer, Serent) (source: tracxn.com; crunchbase.com).

- **First customers & early GTM (most important).**
  - **Founder-led, discovery-driven early sales** with no restaurant network — they earned credibility by embedding with operators (night interviews, inventory counts) and building exactly what mid-size operators needed (source: restaurant365.com/blog).
  - **Target = independents and emerging/multi-unit operators** who had outgrown QuickBooks but were too small for legacy enterprise ERP. Wedge: connected accounting + operations in the cloud, which no incumbent offered.
  - **ROI proof as the sales engine** — "doubled without adding staff." They sold labor/back-office efficiency and food-cost control, not features.
  - **Accountant/bookkeeper channel** became a core scaling motion: a **Certified Accounting Firm Partner program** (Bronze→Platinum tiers), the **R365 Academy** (certification/training), and a referral/implementation partner ecosystem — turning restaurant-specialist CPAs into a distribution and implementation army (source: restaurant365.com/accounting-firms; prnewswire.com).
  - **Community-led brand** — heavy NRA presence plus its own conferences, and a dedicated "Chief Community Officer," as deliberate demand-gen.
  - **Pricing = per-location subscription** (entry "Essential" tier ~$469–499/location/month, higher for Professional) **plus separate implementation fees (~$2,000–$10,000)** and a multi-month rollout. Payroll, Scheduling, and Banking are add-ons (~$30–80/employee/month or flat per-location). Cost-effective above ~$1M/location revenue; the math is marginal for single locations under ~$750K (source: restaurant365.com/pricing; softwareadvice.com).

- **Evolution & pivots.** Revenue **~$73M (2022) → ~$100M (2023) → ~$132M (2024)**; ~**40,000 locations**; ~1,000–1,100 employees. Expanded from accounting into scheduling/labor, payroll/HR, inventory, and banking/fintech — an explicit "supersize the stack" strategy around the 2024 raise. **Struggles:** COVID-related cuts in 2020; and in **2024 laid off ~9% of staff** after missing aggressive growth targets it had staffed up for — the notable "growth decelerated vs. plan" data point despite the $175M raise (source: getlatka.com; techcrunch.com; restaurantbusinessonline.com).

- **Restaurant-specific lessons.**
  - Vertical depth beats horizontal breadth — one industry, fully connected acct+ops, was the founding bet and the moat.
  - Domain immersion substitutes for domain pedigree — non-restaurant founders won by literally counting inventory with operators.
  - ROI narratives ("doubled without adding back-office staff") close deals in a thin-margin, labor-constrained industry better than feature lists.
  - Accounting firms are a force-multiplier channel in restaurants — certify the CPAs who already sit between operators and their books.
  - Pitfall — over-forecasting growth: staffing to an aggressive plan then cutting 9% shows the risk of moving up-market as macro restaurant conditions tightened.
  - Per-location pricing + implementation fees make for a heavier, slower sell — precisely the gap MarginEdge attacked.

- **Sources.** restaurant365.com/about-us; techcrunch.com/2024/05/15/restaurant365-orders-in-175m; restaurant365.com/in-the-news (KKR/L Catterton $135M); prnewswire.com (ICONIQ $88M); restaurant365.com/press/restaurant365-raises-20-million-from-bessemer; restaurantbusinessonline.com/technology/tech-supplier-restaurant365-lays-9-staff; getlatka.com/companies/restaurant365.

## MarginEdge

- **What it is / category.** Restaurant management + back-office automation focused on **invoice/line-item data capture, real-time food-cost/COGS, inventory, recipe costing, ordering, and bill pay**, feeding data into the operator's *existing* accounting system. Positioned as the simple, operator-friendly alternative to R365 for independents and small multi-unit groups. HQ: Arlington, VA (source: marginedge.com).

- **Founders & backgrounds.** **Bo Davis** (co-founder/CEO) — earlier founded ed-tech company **Prometheus** (sold to Blackboard, 2002), then opened **Wasabi**, a conveyor-belt sushi chain (~a dozen US locations, from 2005); MS Finance, London Business School; Peace Corps alum. **Roy Phillips** (co-founder) — Joint Venture Partner at **Bloomin' Brands**, developed the **Bonefish Grill** concept in the mid-Atlantic (15 openings). Plus technologist co-founders (**Brian Mills**, ex-Starfish Retention; also credited Michael Spitalney and Sam Leiber). Bo + Roy bring "40+ years of restaurant experience" (source: marginedge.com/our-story; getmeez.com; crunchbase.com).

- **Founding story.** Founded **2015**. Origin insight is unusually authentic: Bo Davis was an actual owner-operator who suffered the pain of manual invoice entry and inventory in a back-of-kitchen closet, unable to know financial health until month-end. His team built homegrown tools for *daily* visibility; that became MarginEdge. The core product problem: restaurant invoices are "too messy" (many vendors, SKUs, substitutions, handwriting), so turning any invoice into structured line-item data was the wedge. They spent ~3 years and worked with ~20 restaurants before pushing to market — "get it right instead of rushing" (source: marginedge.com/our-story; finance.yahoo.com; techcrunch.com).

- **Funding history.**
  - First round May 2018 (seed/pre-A) (source: tracxn.com).
  - **Series A — $5M, March 2019** — Osage Venture Partners, IrishAngels, Schooner Capital (source: crunchbase.com; osageventurepartners.com).
  - **Series B — $18M, Sept 2021** (source: techcrunch.com).
  - **Series C — $45M, Dec 2022, led by Ten Coves Capital**, with **Fiserv, Derive Ventures** and prior investors — bringing total to **>$70M** (source: techcrunch.com; prnewswire.com).
  - **Total raised ~$71–83M** (press "over $70M"; Tracxn ~$83M over 6 rounds). **Status: private, VC-backed** (no acquisition/IPO). Exact valuations not disclosed.

- **First customers & early GTM (most important).**
  - **Founder-as-target-customer credibility** — "built by restaurant people for restaurant people." Bo's operator background enabled peer-to-peer selling to independents.
  - **Target = independents and small groups** drowning in invoices/food-cost/pen-and-paper. Early customers/case studies: **Wasabi** (dogfooding), maman, Clyde's, Cava Mezze Grill, boloco, Honey Baked Ham, Nara Thai (source: marginedge.com/case-study/wasabi).
  - **Transparent, low-friction pricing as the wedge** — **flat ~$350/location/month, no long-term contract, unlimited invoice processing and unlimited US bill pay**, ~10% annual-prepay discount. Deliberate anti-R365 positioning (no tiered add-ons, no big implementation fee). Toast customers additionally need Toast's ~$50/mo Restaurant Management Suite for API access (source: marginedge.com/pricing; restaurantdive.com).
  - **Integration-led distribution** — complements rather than replaces the operator's accounting: **50+ POS and 15+ accounting integrations**, QuickBooks partnership content — reducing switching friction (source: marginedge.com/how-it-works).
  - **Bill Pay** added a payments/fintech revenue layer and a stickiness hook (operators pay vendors inside the app).

- **Evolution & pivots.** ~**4,000 restaurants** at Series C (Dec 2022, ~$3B/yr invoices processed) → **~11,000–12,000 clients** across US/Canada by 2025–26. Added an **AI suite** in 2025 (sales forecasting, recipe building, invoice automation). No reported layoffs/down-rounds; remains independent (source: techcrunch.com; finance.yahoo.com/small-business; businesswire.com).

- **Restaurant-specific lessons.**
  - Operator-founder authenticity is a moat — living the "invoice-closet" problem shortened early sales cycles with independents.
  - Solving the ugliest manual chore (line-item capture of any invoice within 24–48h) creates immediate, tangible daily value.
  - Simple, flat, no-contract "unlimited" pricing wins thin-margin independents — explicit anti-enterprise positioning.
  - Complement, don't replace, the accountant — integrating with existing POS + accounting lowered adoption friction vs. rip-and-replace ERP.
  - Sell "daily visibility > month-end P&L" — the whole promise is turning a lagging monthly P&L into a live cost/budget view.
  - Pitfall/limit: a flat-fee, independent-focused model caps ACV and pushes it into R365's lane as customers grow multi-unit — hence the move up-market and into bill pay/AI.

- **Sources.** marginedge.com/our-story; techcrunch.com/2022/12/07/marginedge-45m-restaurant-spend; techcrunch.com/2021/09/13/marginedge-...-raises-18m; prnewswire.com (Series C); marginedge.com/pricing; finance.yahoo.com/small-business (Bo Davis "messy" problem).

## MarketMan

- **What it is / category.** Cloud-based **restaurant inventory, purchasing, vendor/supplier management, food costing, and accounts-payable** platform connecting operators with their suppliers. More narrowly inventory/procurement-focused than R365/MarginEdge's full back-office. Origin: Tel Aviv; later NYC HQ (offices London, SF Bay) (source: marketman.com).

- **Founders & backgrounds.** **Noam Wolf** (CEO), **Igal Wolf** (CTO), **Or Mintz** — founded in **Tel Aviv (2012–2013)**. The founders came from **10+ years building mobile apps/technology in Israel** — tech/mobile builders, not restaurant operators (contrast with MarginEdge/Crunchtime) (source: marketman.com/blog; crunchbase.com). *(Note: sources variously date launch 2012 vs 2013; the company itself frames "launched 2013.")*

- **Founding story.** Founded **2012–2013**. Origin insight: restaurant back-of-house was completely overlooked by tech — operators ran on phone/mail/fax order forms, sourced inventory by word of mouth or pen-and-paper, paid suppliers by check, and juggled up to a dozen suppliers with items slipping through the cracks. MarketMan put ordering, inventory, invoices, and food-cost in one cloud place for both operators and suppliers (source: marketman.com/blog/israeli-technology-concept...; prweb.com).

- **Funding history.**
  - **Total independent venture funding ≈ $6.8M** (seed-stage) from lool ventures, Flashpoint Venture Capital, NFX, VentureOut (source: tracxn.com; crunchbase.com).
  - **DATA-CONFLICT FLAG:** the widely-cited "$100M+ raise" is **wrong/conflated** — the **$100M+ came from PSG (PSG Equity)** funding the **Meal Ticket / MarketMan merger**, not capital MarketMan raised independently. The prompt's "Meredith" is a misidentification — the acquirer was **Meal Ticket**, not Meredith (source: psgequity.com; pymnts.com).
  - **Acquisition/status:** **Jan 12, 2022 — merged with / acquired by Meal Ticket**, backed by a **$100M+ growth investment from PSG Equity**. Now a **PE-owned Meal Ticket portfolio product** (source: psgequity.com; pymnts.com; themiddlemarket.com).

- **First customers & early GTM (most important).**
  - **SMB-first, self-serve-leaning.** ICP ≈ 2–5 locations, ~$600K–$3M revenue/unit, already on a modern POS (Toast/Square/Lightspeed), with someone who "owns food cost" (source: capterra.com; restaurantinventorytools.com — inference-heavy).
  - **POS + distributor partnerships as distribution** — deep POS integrations, a **Square** partnership, and distribution via **Performance Foodservice** (a major broadline distributor) — landing customers through the ecosystem rather than heavy enterprise field sales (source: marketman.com; performancefoodservice.com).
  - **Pricing = per-location tiers** — **Starter ~$199/mo, Growth ~$249/mo, Enterprise custom**; volume discounts multi-site. Lower entry price targets budget-sensitive independents (source: marketman.com/pricing; capterra.com).
  - Manages **12,000+ restaurants** worldwide, processing **$40B+** in transactions (company-stated) (source: marketman.com).

- **Evolution & pivots.** Expanded Tel Aviv → global (NYC HQ, London, SF Bay); broadened from inventory into vendor payments and analytics; now one product inside Meal Ticket's broader supplier-side + operator-side foodservice suite, PSG-backed.

- **Restaurant-specific lessons.**
  - A tech-builder team can win a vertical via a sharp wedge — attack the single most-ignored area (back-of-house procurement/inventory) rather than boiling the ocean.
  - Distribution partnerships (POS + broadline distributors) scale SMB acquisition cheaper than enterprise field sales.
  - Low per-location entry pricing lands independents but compresses ACV — a point-solution ceiling.
  - Consolidation is the endgame for narrow point solutions — an inventory-only tool merged into a broader platform to survive against full-stack R365.
  - Watch the data: public aggregators badly conflated MarketMan's ~$6.8M raise with PSG's $100M+ platform investment — verify funding claims against primary press.

- **Sources.** marketman.com/blog/israeli-technology-concept-founded-to-change-the-game-for-restauranteurs; psgequity.com/news (Meal Ticket + MarketMan merger); pymnts.com/2022 (merger); tracxn.com/d/companies/marketman; marketman.com/pricing.

## Crunchtime (CrunchTime! Information Systems)

- **What it is / category.** Enterprise **back-of-house restaurant operations** platform for **large multi-unit chains and franchise systems**: inventory & food-cost, labor management & scheduling, ops execution/task management, food safety, learning/training, and (via acquisitions) kitchen-display/guest management. The enterprise incumbent of this group and the oldest. HQ: Boston (source: crunchtime.com).

- **Founders & backgrounds.** **Bill Bellissimo** — founder & CEO for **~27 years (1995–2023)**; worked in restaurants young, then a Wall Street career, then ~10 years as **President of a regional NE US restaurant chain** before founding Crunchtime. **John Raguin** became CEO in 2023 (formerly co-founder/President of **Guidewire**, enterprise insurance software) to lead the next growth/scaling phase (source: mgmtboston.com; restauranttechnologynews.com; crunchtime.com).

- **Founding story.** Founded **1995**. Running restaurants, Bellissimo grew frustrated with the era's cumbersome systems and "invested heavily to build the tools he wished he'd had" to manage food and labor costs. Early products included **Net-Chef** inventory/labor modules — the same operator-founder pattern as MarginEdge, but two decades earlier and aimed at enterprise (source: restauranttechnologynews.com; mgmtboston.com).

- **Funding history / status.**
  - **Bootstrapped ~20 years** with no announced outside capital — an unusual and important fact (source: mgmtboston.com).
  - **Oct 2015 — Battery Ventures** growth/PE investment (effectively sole institutional investor; majority/control-oriented) (source: battery.com; cooleyma.com).
  - **Status: private, PE-backed (Battery Ventures).** No IPO; valuations undisclosed.

- **Consolidation (its "evolution").**
  - **June 2022 — acquired Zenput** (store-ops execution: food safety, brand standards, task/checklist mgmt; customers included Chipotle, Domino's, P.F. Chang's, Five Guys, 7-Eleven), broadening from back-office into ops execution (source: qsrmagazine.com; globenewswire.com).
  - **June 2025 — announced merger with QSR Automations** (Louisville; ConnectSmart kitchen-display/automation + reservations/guest mgmt). Battery Ventures backs both and is rolling up the stack; CEO John Raguin leads the combined entity. Combined: **800+ brands across 150,000+ locations in 100+ countries, ~90% of the top 50 US chains** (source: restaurantbusinessonline.com; prnewswire.com; pehub.com).

- **First customers & early GTM (most important).**
  - **Enterprise / multi-unit only** — targets chains with 10+ locations, in practice the biggest brands/franchisors. Serves **500+ brands / 125,000+ locations / 100+ countries, ~80% of top-50 US brands**. Named customers: Chipotle, Burger King, Dunkin', Five Guys, Jersey Mike's, McDonald's, P.F. Chang's, The Cheesecake Factory (source: crunchtime.com/our-customers; mgmtboston.com).
  - **Operator credibility landed the first big chains** — Bellissimo's regional-chain-president background gave the domain authority and network for a founder-led, relationship-driven enterprise motion (not self-serve).
  - **Franchise flywheel as GTM** — when a franchisor standardizes on Crunchtime, franchisees roll on; franchisees who adopt it show better food/labor control and brand compliance, making them **top candidates for approval to open new locations** — a compounding land-and-expand loop inside big systems (source: crunchtime.com/franchisee).
  - **ROI / operational-excellence sell** — real-time COGS, food+labor control, multi-site scheduling, single dashboard for 100+-location owners — sold on hard operational ROI to sophisticated buyers.
  - **Pricing = custom/enterprise contracts** (bespoke; no public list price) (source: capterra.com).

- **Restaurant-specific lessons.**
  - Bootstrapping a vertical SaaS to enterprise scale is possible with operator credibility — 20 years, no VC, landing the biggest brands: durability and profitability over blitzscaling.
  - Own the franchisor to win the franchisees — standardize at HQ, then let the system pull the software down to every unit; tie adoption to expansion approvals.
  - Enterprise trust is earned over years — long reference lists become the moat; switching costs at 100k+ locations are enormous.
  - PE + roll-up is the mature-phase playbook — Battery's 2015 investment funded Zenput (2022) and QSR Automations (2025) to build a full "food lifecycle" platform.
  - Leadership succession for scale — replacing a 27-year founder-CEO with an enterprise-software operator (ex-Guidewire) signals a deliberate shift to institutional scaling.
  - Pitfall/contrast: strong up-market but structurally ill-suited to independents — which is exactly why the SMB back-office lane went to R365/MarginEdge/MarketMan.

- **Sources.** mgmtboston.com/crunchtime; restauranttechnologynews.com/2022/06/spotlight-interview-bill-bellissimo; battery.com/company/crunchtime; qsrmagazine.com (Zenput); prnewswire.com / restaurantbusinessonline.com (QSR Automations merger 2025); crunchtime.com/franchisee; crunchtime.com/our-customers.

# PART B — BI / FORECASTING / SCHEDULING

## Lineup.ai

- **What it is / category.** AI-powered restaurant **demand forecasting + labor scheduling**. Ingests POS history and blends it with external signals (weather, local events, reservations) to produce daily/weekly sales, labor, and menu-item forecasts, feeding scheduling tools. Integrates with Toast, Square, Heartland, PAR Brink, NCR Aloha, Clover (source: lineup.ai; predicthq.com/customers/lineup).

- **Founders & backgrounds — IMPORTANT STRUCTURAL DISTINCTION.** Lineup.ai is **not a classic founder-led startup** — it was **built inside a corporate venture studio, 1848 Ventures, LLC, backed by Westfield Insurance** (Ohio P&C insurer). "Founding" credit belongs to the 1848 Ventures team. **CEO: Sam (Samuel) Gerace** (serial entrepreneur; per PitchBook/Grokipedia — *moderate confidence, not a primary bio*). A "Boone Putney" appears as an engineering/CTO figure but **could not be confirmed from a primary source — flag as unverified** (source: lineup.ai/about-us; crainscleveland.com; pitchbook.com).

- **Founding story.** Founded **2020**, HQ Chicago (ties to 1848 Ventures/Westfield in Ohio). Origin insight: external factors — starting with **weather** — materially move restaurant sales and can be modeled better than operators' crude tools. **Best-documented early proof-of-concept:** a pilot with **five restaurants clustered near Chicago's United Center**; using scheduled arena events + historical sales/weather/reservations, they built an ML model and **in four weeks showed it beat the industry-standard 4-week rolling average** "in many cases significantly" (source: predicthq.com/customers/lineup; lineup.ai).

- **Funding history — KEY DISTINCTION.** **No independent venture rounds** — funded internally by 1848 Ventures/Westfield as a studio portfolio company. Searches for any external seed/Series A returned nothing, consistent with an internally-funded build. **Exit: Aug 6, 2024 — acquired by TimeForge** (restaurant/retail labor-management vendor); **terms undisclosed**. Effectively a ~4-year studio build to a modest strategic exit (source: timeforge.com; qsrmagazine.com).

- **First customers & early GTM (most important).**
  - **Pilot-driven, ROI-proof GTM** — the United Center five-restaurant pilot was explicitly designed to prove ROI vs. a known baseline (beat the 4-week rolling average) before scaling.
  - **Target = independent and small multi-unit groups (SMBs), not big chains.** Reps ran a high-volume outbound motion (~100+ calls/day) — **founder/studio-led high-touch outbound**, not channel/PLG at first (source: LinkedIn rep profiles — inference).
  - **Flagship ROI case study — Carrot Express** (17-location fast-casual) cut payroll ~300 basis points, projected **$1.1–1.3M annual savings** (source: timeforge.com; grokipedia.com).
  - **POS marketplaces as a channel** — listed on Toast and Square app marketplaces. Pricing not disclosed.

- **Evolution & pivots.** 2020 studio launch → weather/event forecasting POC → productized sales+labor+menu forecasting with POS integrations → **acquired by TimeForge (Aug 2024)**, where the forecasting engine now lives inside TimeForge's labor suite. Modest build-to-strategic-fit outcome, not a venture-scale independent (source: qsrmagazine.com).

- **Restaurant-specific lessons.**
  - Beat the incumbent baseline explicitly — "we beat the 4-week rolling average" (the tool operators already trust) is a concrete, verifiable claim; better than abstract "AI."
  - Anchor pilots to a demand driver you can prove (a sports/events venue) so the forecast is visibly better.
  - Quantify ROI in operator language — basis points of payroll, dollars saved/year (Carrot Express $1.1–1.3M) — not model-accuracy metrics.
  - SMB restaurant sales is a grind (100+ dials/day); a point forecasting tool may be too thin a wedge to stand alone — hence the exit into a broader labor platform.
  - Corporate-studio funding gives runway without dilution but caps ambition — a build-to-strategic-fit path, not a rocket.

- **Sources.** lineup.ai/about-us; predicthq.com/customers/lineup; timeforge.com/press-releases (TimeForge adds Lineup.ai); qsrmagazine.com; crainscleveland.com (1848 Ventures).

## Tenzo

- **What it is / category.** UK **restaurant analytics / business-intelligence** platform for multi-site hospitality operators. Pulls POS, labor/scheduling, inventory, reviews, and delivery data into one real-time reporting layer, plus ML sales forecasting and a natural-language assistant ("TenzoGPT") (source: gotenzo.com; techcrunch.com).

- **Founders & backgrounds.** **Christian Mouysset (CEO)** and **Adam Taylor (CTO)** — met studying Computer Science at Cambridge. Christian **co-founded and grew the London chain Hummus Bros to six locations** — living the exact pain Tenzo solves (data siloed across tools, rekeyed into Excel). Adam came from video-game engineering (Tony Hawk's Pro Skater, Final Fantasy) then **McKinsey's big-data team** (source: techstars.com; gotenzo.com). *(The prompt's "Christoffer Thomsen" is not a Tenzo founder — corrected.)*

- **Founding story.** Founded in **London, 2016**. Origin insight: a restaurant's data lives in a dozen disconnected systems that head office rekeys into Excel so GMs can see how to make a branch profitable; Tenzo automates that into real-time reporting. Accepted into **Techstars London 2016** (~$212K) (source: techstars.com; gotenzo.com/company-about-us).

- **Funding history.**
  - **Techstars 2016 — ~$212K** (source: leadiq.com).
  - **Seed — April 2017 — ~£600K (~$742K)**, led by Acequia Capital and Force Over Mass, with angels incl. **Charlie Songhurst** (ex-Microsoft) and **Getti Restaurants** (a customer-investor) (source: techcrunch.com/2017/04/10/tenzo; uktech.news).
  - **Nov 2020 — Innovate UK grant** (non-dilutive; food-waste tech).
  - **Series A — May 2023 — $5M (~£4M)**, co-led by Amadeus Capital Partners and S28 Capital (source: amadeuscapital.com; crunchbase.com).
  - **Series A extension — April 2026 — $5M first close**, led by Edge.vc with Amadeus, YourVC, and customer-investor Frontive Holdings (source: amadeuscapital.com; ffnews.com).
  - **Total raised ~$12.5M → ~$15M** post-2026 extension. **Valuation undisclosed. Status: independent, operating** (~37 employees, ~$3.5M revenue 2024 per GetLatka — *aggregator estimate*).

- **Restaurant-specific lessons.**

  - Ride the shift-work tailwind but respect its cyclicality — hospitality digitization accelerated the rebound, but casual labor is volatile (closures/seasonality).

Flagship **Flippy** — an autonomous robotic arm originally built to flip burgers, re-focused on the fry station (Flippy ROAR / Flippy 2 / Flippy 3).   - RaaS exists because restaurants won't pay CapEx — but it parks the hardware cost on the startup's balance sheet.
  - Integration, reliability, and field service are silent killers — kitchens are hot/greasy/cramped/non-uniform; retrofitting is bespoke per site (hence outsourcing to Roboworx).
  - Sister-company first customers (CaliBurger) inflate early traction that doesn't generalize to arm's-length buyers.

Pitch: a running robot cheaper than added FOH labor, improving table turns (source: nrn.com; restaurantdive.com; robotlab.com).

- **Evolution & pivots.** Product line broadened (Servi family) and expanded from restaurants into hotels/hospitality (elevator-integrated delivery roadmap accelerated post-LG). Trajectory: SoftBank-backed AMR startup → global scale (thousands of units, strong Asia presence) → **absorbed into LG** as its commercial-robot arm. *(No confirmed layoffs/down-round found — the LG deal was framed as continuity, not distress; but a $600M valuation vs ~$175M raised is a modest step-up, not a blowout. Flag: layoff status unverified.)* (source: techcrunch.com; bearrobotics.ai).

- **Restaurant-specific lessons.**
  - FOH delivery robots have far better unit economics than kitchen robots — simpler task, no food manipulation, cheaper hardware (~$399/mo vs Miso's ~$3–5K/mo), driving broader real deployment.
  - RaaS + short demo lowers trial friction — a 28-day demo and a sub-$1K/month lease fit restaurant budgets better than CapEx.
  - Asia (Japan/Korea) adopted faster than the US — chain density (Denny's Japan) and cultural acceptance matter.
  - Even a "success" needed a conglomerate parent — LG's manufacturing/distribution scale, not standalone VC economics, is what carried Bear.
  - Pitch table-turns and labor redeployment, not replacement — the ROI is servers focusing on guests while the robot runs food.

- **Sources.** techcrunch.com/2024/03/12 (LG $60M); techcrunch.com/2025/01/24 (LG majority, ~$600M); therobotreport.com (Series B $81M); businesswire.com; nrn.com; bearrobotics.ai/company-history; robotlab.com (pricing).

## Chef Robotics

- **What it is / category.** **AI-powered food-assembly robotics** — robotic arms with an AI food-manipulation platform (**ChefOS**) that scoop/portion ingredients into meal trays. **Crucially NOT a restaurant company** — it serves **high-mix food manufacturers** (prepared-meal/meal-kit/airline/school-lunch producers), where ~60–70% of labor is manual ingredient assembly. Deliberate "anti-restaurant" positioning (source: chefrobotics.ai; techcrunch.com; agfundernews.com).

- **Founders & backgrounds.** Founded **2019** by **Rajat Bhageria** (CEO) — previously founder/CEO of **ThirdEye** (CV wearable for the visually impaired) and founder/MP of **Prototype Capital** (pre-seed VC for "new tech for old industries"); Penn Robotics & ML Lab master's; Wharton-affiliated (source: chefrobotics.ai; agfundernews.com; forbes.com).

- **Founding story & the pivotal insight.** Chef **initially targeted fast-casual restaurants but turned those customers away.** Bhageria concluded that (1) restaurants demanded a full human-equivalent system (handle every ingredient) or it wasn't worth it — an all-or-nothing bar; (2) the training data to build a "pick up anything" food robot doesn't exist, and food is deformable/sticky/wet with no good simulators; (3) **food manufacturing solved both problems** — ~60–70% of labor is repetitive assembly, so you can *partially* automate and still add value, while high-volume repetition generates the real-world training data that improves ChefOS (a data flywheel). Emerged from stealth after demos at Amy's Kitchen (source: techcrunch.com/2025/04/10; spectrum.ieee.org; agfundernews.com).

- **Funding history.**
  - **Seed — $11.2M, March 2023**, led by Construct Capital (Kleiner Perkins, Promus, Bloomberg Beta, Gaingels).
  - **Series A — $43.1M, announced April 2025**, led by **Avataar Venture Partners** — notably split **$20.6M equity + $22.5M equipment-financing debt** (via Silicon Valley Bank/First Citizens), with the debt **explicitly earmarked to fund the robots under RaaS so customers pay no CapEx** — the cleanest example of RaaS shifting the balance-sheet burden onto the vendor/its lenders.
  - **Total raised ~$65.6M** (~$38.8M equity + ~$26.75M equipment financing). Chef declines to disclose revenue but claims strong unit economics (*company-stated, unverified*) — far less capital-bloated than Miso (source: prnewswire.com; cnbc.com; therobotreport.com; agfundernews.com).

- **First customers & GTM (most important).**
  - **Marquee customers:** Amy's Kitchen, Chef Bombay, Sunbasket, Cafe Spice, plus "one of the largest school-lunch providers in the country."
  - **Scale:** robots produced 45M+ meals (2025) → 100M+ servings (2026); dozens of robots across the US (expanding to UK/Canada).
  - **RaaS pricing:** small upfront deployment fee + ongoing monthly/quarterly subscription (per-customer terms); no large CapEx — financed by Chef via the SVB equipment-debt facility.
  - **Quantified ROI (customer-cited):** Chef Bombay −88% food giveaway, +33% labor productivity; Amy's Kitchen +12% consistency, +17% labor productivity; Sunbasket ~10% fewer people/line, +17% throughput (*vendor/customer-reported, not independently audited*) (source: chefrobotics.ai/case-studies; techcrunch.com; agfundernews.com).

- **Evolution.** Positioned as having "escaped the robot-cooking graveyard" by avoiding restaurants — explicitly contrasting with failed restaurant-robot startups (Zume, Spyce, Kernel, implicitly Miso). Core remains high-mix food manufacturing, exploring adjacencies (ghost kitchens) (source: techcrunch.com/2026/04/17; foodondemand.com).

- **Restaurant-specific lessons.**
  - Food is physically hard for robots; full autonomy is the wrong bar — restaurants demanded all-or-nothing, which is unbuildable given no training data/simulators.
  - Partial automation in high-volume, repetitive settings (manufacturing) adds value immediately AND generates the data flywheel — which is why the manufacturing-focused player thrives while restaurant-focused peers struggle.
  - RaaS financed with dedicated equipment debt is the cleanest capital structure — segregate the CapEx-heavy hardware into a debt facility rather than burning equity.
  - The most successful "restaurant robotics" company succeeded by leaving restaurants — a sobering lesson for anyone selling robots directly into restaurant kitchens.

- **Sources.** techcrunch.com/2025/04/10 (turning away original customers); techcrunch.com/2026/04/17 (escaped the graveyard); cnbc.com/2025/03/31; prnewswire.com ($43M Series A); agfundernews.com; spectrum.ieee.org; chefrobotics.ai/case-studies.

## Cross-cutting observations (this batch)

1. **Operator-founder credibility (or deep customer immersion) is the single strongest early-GTM asset in restaurants.** MarginEdge (Bo Davis/Wasabi), Crunchtime (Bellissimo, ex-chain president), Tenzo (Mouysset/Hummus Bros), 7shifts (Boesch's dad's Quiznos), Deputy (Ahmed's Hungry Jack's) all sold peer-to-peer on lived pain. Where founders lacked restaurant pedigree (R365, MarketMan), the winner (R365) manufactured credibility by literally counting inventory with operators; the substitute for pedigree is obsessive customer discovery.

2. **Hard-dollar ROI on the two controllable costs — food and labor — is the universal sales narrative.** Every ops/BI company sells in operator language: R365's "doubled without adding back-office staff," MarginEdge's "daily P&L vs month-end," Lineup's "beat the 4-week rolling average / $1.1M payroll saved," Tenzo's "−80% food waste / +15% labor productivity," Deputy's "roster in 5 minutes." Model-accuracy metrics don't sell; basis-points-of-payroll and dollars-saved do.

3. **Chain-vs-independent is the market's primary fault line, and it dictates GTM.** Independents/SMBs are won by **PLG/self-serve + simple flat pricing** (7shifts $1/employee, Homebase freemium, MarginEdge $350 flat, Deputy's 0→70% self-serve pivot). Chains/franchisors are won by **founder-led/relationship enterprise sales + the franchisor→franchisee flywheel** (Crunchtime; R365 moving up-market). Deputy and R365 explicitly run *both* motions in parallel — PLG for the long tail, sales for multi-unit. Trying to sell independents with an enterprise motion (or vice-versa) is where companies stall.

4. **Prove value with a small, baseline-beating pilot before scaling.** Lineup's 5-restaurant United Center POC (beat the rolling average in 4 weeks), MarginEdge's 3-year/20-restaurant build, R365's "fourth customer" proof, and the robotics pilots all show restaurants buy on demonstrated ROI, not promises. The corollary in robotics is the cautionary tale: pilots that generate PR but **never scale** (White Castle's 100→13).

5. **Point solutions consolidate; full-stack platforms expand organically.** Narrow tools get rolled up or absorbed — MarketMan into Meal Ticket (PSG), Lineup.ai into TimeForge, Zenput + QSR Automations into Crunchtime (Battery). Full-stack players (R365) and daily-workflow owners (7shifts, Homebase, Deputy) expand organically into payroll/banking/AI. A single-module restaurant SaaS has a structural ACV ceiling and eventually needs to bundle or be bundled.

6. **Owning a daily, employee-touching workflow beats owning a periodic analytics report.** Scheduling/time-clock (7shifts, Homebase, Deputy) is used every day by every employee, creating virality inside the venue and natural expansion into payroll/tips. Pure BI/forecasting (Tenzo, Lineup) is used weekly by one manager — capital-efficient but slow-compounding (Tenzo ~$3.5M revenue nine years in; Lineup exited thin). If you build a point analytics tool, attach it to a daily workflow or a broader platform.

7. **Restaurant end-markets are thin-margin and macro-sensitive — over-raising at a peak is punished.** 7shifts (two layoff rounds, 7% then 19%, after its $80M SoftBank peak) and R365 (~9% layoff after missing staffed-up growth targets) both show that raising at frothy valuations sets a cost base that inflation-hit restaurants can't support. Conservative growth assumptions and capital efficiency (Deputy bootstrapped 8 years to 28,000 customers; Crunchtime 20 years no VC; Tenzo ~$15M total) age better than blitzscaling in this vertical.

8. **Restaurant robotics is a category apart — capital-intensive, slow to adopt, and economically unforgiving.** All three robotics firms raised far more than they earn (Miso >$100M vs ~$385K revenue; Bear ~$175M; Chef ~$66M). RaaS exists solely because restaurants refuse CapEx, but it dumps the hardware cost onto the vendor's balance sheet (Chef literally raised $22.5M of equipment debt to fund customer robots). The "wins" are telling: **Bear needed a hardware conglomerate (LG) to absorb it; Chef succeeded only by abandoning restaurants for manufacturing; Miso survives on retail crowdfunding, not institutional VC or an IPO.** FOH delivery (Bear, ~$399/mo, simple task) adopts far better than kitchen manipulation (Miso, ~$3–5K/mo, greasy bespoke retrofits). Integration, reliability, and field service — not the demo — are what kill kitchen robots at scale.

9. **Compliance and payments are the two most reliable ways to deepen a restaurant relationship.** Deputy's Australian award interpretation is a defensible moat; Homebase, R365, and 7shifts all moved into payroll; MarginEdge and MarketMan added bill pay. Once a restaurant runs payroll or pays vendors through your platform, switching costs and ARPU jump. Scheduling/invoices are the wedge; compliance and money movement are the retention.

10. **Strategic/industry investors buy credibility, not just capital.** Danny Meyer's Enlightened Hospitality (7shifts), Fiserv (MarginEdge), Ecolab (Miso), and customer-investors (Getti/Frontive in Tenzo; Express Employment in Deputy) function as trust signals to restaurant buyers in a tight-knit, reference-driven industry — often more valuable than the dollars.
