# AZ Restaurant Partners — Breadth, the Moat, and the Honest Signal Read

*Written after reading the 56-company founder research (`research/founder-playbook/`), the flagship
capability ledger + case study (`flagship/`), the growth engine (`growth_engine/`), and what's live
(`command/worklog.json`, `experiments.json`, `llms.txt`). This is the cofounder answer to two questions:
(1) is the breadth an asset or a liability, and (2) what is the actual moat.*

---

## 1. Breadth: asset or liability? — "Sell one number, deliver the platform."

**The research verdict is unambiguous: no winner started broad; every winner ended broad.**
Toast started as a POS, Square as one card reader, OpenTable as a reservation book, Owner.com as a
website+ordering, Loop as fee-recovery. Then every category leader became a multi-product platform
(Toast, Square, SpotOn, Owner, R365, Popmenu). **Breadth is the endgame, not the entry.**

So breadth is not one thing — it's three, and they have different verdicts:

| Kind of breadth | Verdict | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Breadth of *delivery*** (you do A→Z for the client) | **Your single biggest ASSET** | The customer's #1 pain *is* fragmentation — a dozen tools that don't talk. A point-product can't be the cure; you can. "One team, one throat to choke." For a services+automation business, breadth = higher LTV and stickiness (you become their tech department). The deepest moats in the research owned the *widest* surface (Toast). |
| **Breadth of *message*** (the first pitch says "we do everything") | **A LIABILITY — fix this** | Every winner led the first conversation with ONE quantified wedge and expanded after. Your site/onepager currently lead with "one platform across every layer." That's breadth-first messaging the research warns against for the first touch. Lead with the *dollar* (delivery commissions), close, then reveal the platform. |
| **Breadth of *build-focus*** (you're building 17 capabilities + side projects) | **The real risk to watch** | The ledger lists 17 capabilities; the repo also carries a stocks project, an employee-handbook app, a chat app, an MCP server, etc. The danger isn't the client offering — it's spreading *your own* build energy thin. 2–3 of these are the moat; the rest are features or distractions. |

**Bottom line:** breadth of delivery is the asset and the core of the moat. Breadth of message and
breadth of build-focus are the two liabilities. The discipline is: **sell one number, deliver the
platform, and build only the 2–3 things no one else can.**

## 2. The moat — it is NOT the tool list. It's a compounding flywheel no competitor can occupy.

The public tool directory / comparison pages are great top-of-funnel SEO, but they are **not** a moat
(anyone can list tools). The real moat is four interlocking parts that form a flywheel:

**(a) The founder fusion — ex-Google engineer + owner of a profitable, award-winning restaurant.**
The 56-company research proved this archetype wins *and* that outsider-founders spent years faking
domain credibility (Toast shadowed staff hundreds of hours; R365's founder counted inventory after
close). You have both natively. Non-replicable, and it's the cheapest trust that exists.

**(b) A live, profitable restaurant as a *permanent* R&D lab (Wok & Karahi).** This is the killer, and
almost nobody has it. You don't pitch ideas — you run them on a real restaurant first, get real numbers
(4.6★ / 872 reviews ≈ doubled from ~400; tablet consolidation live 1yr+; the per-order-type thank-you
card that converts 3P customers to direct, live 1yr+), *then* productize and literally **port** to
clients (the AZ growth engine is "ported from the Wok & Karahi seo_engine"). This is the research's
"prove it in one unit" pattern — but **permanent and owned**, not a client pilot. It means:
every claim is dogfooded (trust), your marginal cost to build a new capability is ~zero (you build it
for yourself anyway), and you get the perfect hook: *"if it's true for us, it's worse for you."*

**(c) The closed-loop autonomous growth engine — a *product* wearing a service's clothes.** Agencies
run growth by hand and bill retainers for slides; they don't scale past headcount. You built software
that senses (GA4/search) → diagnoses → emails you ≤3 proposals → on your approval **auto-commits and
redeploys**, with real guardrails (fail-closed allowlist, scope fence, validate-before-commit,
revertible commits), for ~$10–35/mo of compute. The research is explicit: **software that owns a daily
workflow is the moat; periodic-report tools lose.** This engine is what turns your hours into leverage
— the line between a consultancy (caps at your time) and a platform (scales).

**(d) The frontier SKUs no one offers independents — the self-optimizing menu + the unified CDP /
receipt-scan spin-wheel.** A menu that reads real demand (POS + search + timing + deal performance) and
re-prices/merchandises itself *with owner approval and A/B proof* has **no accessible competitor for
independents** (dynamic menu pricing exists only at enterprise scale). The CDP + point-of-receipt
3P→direct spin-wheel (capture the app's own customer at every order) is likewise novel for independents.
These are the 10x pieces, not incremental ones.

**Why it's a moat and not just a feature list:** it's a *flywheel*. Founder fusion → the restaurant lab
proves capabilities cheaply → they get productized into automation → deployed to Houston independents →
their data makes the automation smarter → more proof → more capabilities. Each turn compounds.
- A **pure agency** can't build the software.
- A **pure SaaS** can't dogfood on a real profitable restaurant, and won't sell founder-to-founder,
  cross-vendor, "your side only," locally.
- A **POS vendor** won't do the human, multi-vendor, no-commission work — their incentive is lock-in.
You sit in a gap none of them can structurally occupy: **the only party that is simultaneously an
operator, an engineer, and vendor-neutral.**

## 3. What's *really* differentiated (beyond the tool list) — ranked

1. **"Dogfooded on a real restaurant, then ported to you."** The single most defensible, most trustworthy
   thing you own. Lead every proof conversation with it.
2. **The closed-loop growth engine as a monthly product** ("we watch your data + the world, propose
   weekly, you approve by email, we ship"). The strongest recurring-retainer offer in the portfolio and
   the thing that scales you.
3. **The self-optimizing menu** — the most differentiated *future* SKU; no competitor for independents.
4. **The CDP + receipt-scan spin-wheel** — a real customer-data platform for independents + on-the-spot
   3P→direct capture.
5. **The AI phone agent** — table stakes emerging, but yours is real-write-capable (28 tools, POS-
   integrated, multilingual) and, once the line is live, a costless proof demo.

## 4. The honest signal read — what's actually true today

**Strong proof-of-VALUE. Zero proof-of-DEMAND yet.** Be clear-eyed about this distinction.

- ✅ **Proof of value (real):** the lab works. Year-plus, real-world results at Wok & Karahi (reviews
  ~doubled to 872 at 4.6★; tablet consolidation; direct-conversion cards; ~81.5k GBP views in H1). The
  method demonstrably creates value on a restaurant.
- ⚠️ **Proof of demand (not yet):** **no external paying restaurant client has run the playbook.** All
  results are on your own restaurant. The AZ site only just became crawlable (GSC verified, 124-URL
  sitemap fetched, a hero A/B test running since 2026-07-07) — so there's no AZ traffic/conversion
  signal yet either.
- The GA4 growth-engine loop is built but **not producing signal until two owner steps are done**
  (grant the service account access to the AZ GA4 property + add the reused secrets) — see
  `growth_engine/PLAN.md`.

**The one tell that changes everything:** the **first external Founding-5 restaurant** running the
playbook, producing **one clean before/after number** (e.g., "direct orders 12% → 34% in 60 days,
$X/mo off the apps"). That single number converts "this works for us" into "this is a business" — and
it's the exact asset the case study is missing (`[OWNER]` brackets). **Getting that is priority #1;
everything else is preparation.**

## 5. Focus / kill list (protect the crown jewels)

**Double down (the leverage + the differentiation):**
- The **closed-loop growth engine** — it's what scales you; wire its GA4 loop so it starts producing signal.
- The **self-optimizing menu** and **CDP/spin-wheel** — the "no one else does this" moat; finish and demo them.
- **Land ONE external client** and capture the before/after number.
- **Get the voice line live** — it's a free, undeniable proof demo (currently paused on go-live).

**Park or kill (distractions from the moat):**
- The **stocks/** project — unrelated; it's spending build energy the moat needs.
- The **employee-handbook** and **chat** microapps — nice re-skins, not moat; offer only if a client asks.
- **More half-built capabilities** — resist. The ledger is already ahead of the proof; convert existing
  ones to a paying client before adding new ones.

**Fix the message (cheap, high-impact):**
- Lead the site/onepager/first-pitch with the **one quantified wedge** (the delivery-commission dollar,
  via the calculator), then reveal the platform. Keep the breadth for delivery, not the opener.

## 6. What to measure — "watch what's working"

Once the GA4 loop is wired, watch:
1. **The wedge:** calculator completions → call bookings. Is the "found-money" number the thing that
   converts? (The research says it will be — instrument it and confirm.)
2. **Which top-of-funnel wins:** guides vs tool-detail pages vs comparison pages — which drive the call.
3. **The hero A/B test** (`experiments.json`) — value/audience-fit hero vs pain-question hero.
4. **Voice-line demo engagement** once live — call-CTA clicks → actual test calls.
5. **The one that matters most:** time-to-first-external-client and their before/after delta.

**The pattern to expect (from the research):** the boring, in-person, one-number wedge will outperform
the broad platform pitch. Sell the number; the platform is what makes them stay.
