The model, plainly

No subscription. No hardware. No staff training. No discounting your menu to strangers on an app.

1 · We fill the table

We recruit locals through community channels, take applications, vet and balance the table, and collect a deposit that commits each guest to showing up on your slowest night.

2 · We host the night

Our host greets guests, seats them, runs two light icebreakers, and stays through the evening. Your team just does what it already does: serve great food.

3 · You pay per seated guest

A flat fee (typically $6 per seated guest) — counted at the door, invoiced the next morning with a one-page recap: covers, spend, and revenue vs. your normal night. No-shows cost you nothing. Your first event is free.

Good questions owners ask

"Is this a dating thing in my dining room?"

No. It's a hosted dinner party. Singles night is one format of four — and even that one looks like a normal long-table reservation: quiet, adult, well-run. No gimmicks, no name tags, nothing your regulars would blink at.

"What does my staff have to do?"

Nothing new. It's one big reservation with a pre-set table plan. Our host handles everything social. Most kitchens actually prefer it — orders arrive in a predictable wave on an otherwise dead night.

"What if guests don't show?"

Deposits cut no-shows hard, and we overbook against a waitlist. Either way, you pay per seated guest — an empty chair never costs you a dollar.

"How do I know it worked?"

Next-morning recap: guests seated, average spend, event-window revenue vs. your same-weekday baseline. If the math doesn't clearly work, stop — no contract, no minimums.
